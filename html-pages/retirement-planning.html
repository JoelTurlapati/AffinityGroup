Income Planning
Step One

Building Your Retirement Income Portfolio

The most important question in retirement is not how much you have saved — it is how much your portfolio will reliably send you each month, and for how long. We build a detailed projection that shows exactly how your assets, Social Security, pension income, and other sources combine to support your lifestyle.

  • Monthly and annual income needs analysis
  • Social Security timing and claiming strategy

  • Portfolio income design using our proven bucket methodology
Tax Strategy
Step Two

Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Planning

Taxes do not stop when your paycheck does. How you draw from your accounts — and in what order — has enormous impact on how long your money lasts. We coordinate with our network of CPAs to craft a withdrawal strategy that minimizes your lifetime tax burden.

  • Roth conversion strategies before Required Minimum Distributions begin
  • Tax-efficient sequencing across taxable, tax-deferred, and tax-free accounts
  • Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) planning from IRAs
  • RMD planning and coordination with your overall income picture

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the questions we hear most from clients approaching or living in retirement

The honest answer is: it depends on your specific lifestyle, expenses, healthcare needs, and income from Social Security or a pension. A common rule of thumb is 10–12 times your annual income, but that number means very little without a personalized projection. We build a detailed cash flow model that shows exactly what you need your portfolio to generate monthly — and whether your current savings trajectory gets you there.
Social Security can be claimed as early as age 62, but every year you delay past your full retirement age increases your benefit by approximately 8%. For many clients, waiting until 70 is worth hundreds of thousands of dollars over a 25–30 year retirement. However, health, cash flow needs, spousal benefits, and other income sources all factor in. This is one of the most impactful decisions in retirement and should not be made without a full analysis.
The 4% rule suggests withdrawing no more than 4% of your portfolio annually to make it last 30 years. It is a useful starting point, but it does not account for your specific tax situation, Social Security income, healthcare costs, or investment mix. We use a more dynamic approach — our bucket methodology — that allocates conservative holdings for near-term income needs and growth-oriented investments for longer horizons, adjusting over time as your situation evolves.
Medicare enrollment begins at age 65 and has specific enrollment windows that can result in permanent penalties if missed. Part A covers hospital care, Part B covers outpatient care, Part D covers prescriptions, and you will also need to choose between traditional Medicare with a Medigap supplement or a Medicare Advantage plan. Your income also affects your Part B and D premiums through IRMAA surcharges. We walk through all of this with our insurance specialists well before your 65th birthday.
Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) are mandatory annual withdrawals from traditional IRAs, 401(k)s, and most other tax-deferred retirement accounts, beginning at age 73 under current law. Failure to take your full RMD results in a 25% penalty on the amount not withdrawn. RMDs can significantly affect your tax bracket and Medicare premiums, which is why we plan for them years in advance — often using Roth conversions in the years before RMDs begin to reduce their future impact.
The worst thing that can happen to a retiree is being forced to sell investments at a loss to cover living expenses. Our bucket methodology directly addresses this: we maintain conservative, low-risk holdings specifically for the next 4–5 years of income needs, so you are never a forced seller during a market downturn. The longer-horizon bucket can remain in growth assets, giving your portfolio time to recover from any short-term volatility.

In many cases, keeping a low-rate mortgage while keeping capital invested is the more wealth-efficient choice. In other cases — particularly when reducing fixed monthly expenses matters for peace of mind — paying it off can make excellent sense. There is no universal answer.
